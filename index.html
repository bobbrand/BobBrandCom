Building Cultures

Is your work fun? Is the environnement collaborative? Does it foster innovation? Are engineers growing and learning while they work? Is the product quality good? Do projects complete on time? Do people work hard? Do they work smart? Do people learn and grow from mistakes? These are only a few of the characteristics that comprise an engineering culture and no two are ever the same.

Engineering Leadership plays a huge role in developing the culture of an engineering organization. Leaders often drive behaviors through their own example. They set priorities for an organization. They recognize and reward behaviors while dissuading other behaviors. It is an important role of a leader, and smart leaders will proactively drive healthy cultures. How is it done?

Data Driven

When I was in school, I had the opportunity to study computer usability which employed quantitative analysis to measure the effectiveness of human computer interaction. In one lecture, my professor told an anecdote about a survey in which employees were asked what food they wanted to see in the vending machine at work.

There was an overwhelming response requesting healthier options, including fresh fruits and low sugar alternatives to traditional snacks. But after the vending machine was restocked with healthy alternatives, the sales reflected that consumers consistently chose the traditional chips and candy bars when they purchased snacks from the machine.

The takeaway of the story, he explained, is that the employees indeed wanted to "see " these options in the vending machine, but that their behavior showed that they wanted to purchase the traditional snacks. Importantly, it also demonstrates the fallibility of qualitative data and the importance of acting on actual behavior

Data permeates the modern software world from digital marketing and product management to diagnostics and software quality.

Connected Devices

Eric Schmidt is credited with coining Mobile First Design at the 2010 Mobile World Congress. Watch Here

Mobile First Design recognized the growing importance of mobile computing, ensuring products work perfectly for mobile users before designing for web and PC users. Anyone who lives or works with someone from Gen-Z will wonder how long it will take before Mobile First Design evolves into Mobile Only Design. In fact, for many services it already has.

But Mobile First Design is just an artifact of a broader movement in mobile computing, and the mobile device has become the gateway for connecting the everyday things in our lives to our online world of cloud services and social communities. It can add extraordinary value to ordinary things, but it is not without its technical challenges.
